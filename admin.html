Script
Cold call reference for reps — the 3 Tens, phase breakdown, and follow-up emails.
Cold call script
Straight line approach — we steer the call, we don't ask permission to steer it.
The 3 tens
Nobody says yes until they hit a 10 out of 10 on all three of these, at the same time. Everything below is built to move all three at once.
1. The productThe actual solution to their problem.
2. YouThey trust you, believe you've got their back.
3. Boring projectThe company behind you is legit.
Before the script starts — human connection
Untimed. This happens first, every call, before anything below it. The 60 seconds doesn't start until you cut to the chase.
Hey, is this [Lead Name]? — wait for yes

Perfect, you're just the person I was looking for! How's your day going? — let them answer, actually listen

Oh good, same here. I'm going to cut to the chase—
Why no company name yet A name they don't recognize is the fastest trigger for a hang up. This part is just two people talking, no identification of who's calling or why. That reveal waits until the timed script below, once the door's already open.

Why this still counts as steering Confirming who you're talking to isn't optional information, and "how's your day" is a genuine human moment capped by however long they naturally take to answer. You're still the one who ends it. Cutting to the chase is announced, not requested.
Broken down, part by part
The same script, one phase at a time, so you can see what each piece is doing before running it as one continuous push.
Straight line step 1 — the hook
"I'd like to properly introduce myself — this is [Your Name], I'm with Boring Project, we help local service businesses in San Antonio get found online, and stay found. This will only take sixty seconds. I found something on Google you need to know about."
Why this works Sharp, enthusiastic, expert, in the first four seconds. No "how are you," no small talk. "This will only take sixty seconds" is a statement, not a question, we're not asking if now's a good time.
Alternate opener you drafted: "I'm with a local company here in San Antonio called Boring Project, a local web agency that builds and manages websites and online visibility for local service businesses like yours." More explicit up front, costs about 16 extra words. Either works, pick whichever feels more natural once you've said it out loud a few times.
Straight line step 2 — intelligence
"I searched "[service] San Antonio" this morning and you didn't show up on the first page. Your competitors did."
Why this works A fact, not a question. In a full conversation you'd ask and listen, here there's no time, so we state the pain point directly and move.
Straight line step 3 — presentation
"Every day, someone in this city searches for exactly what you do. Right now, that call is going to someone else. Not because they're better. Just because they show up first. Think of it like your storefront sign is missing. People are walking by every day, they just can't find you."
Why this works Benefits, not features. Nobody cares about SEO as a concept, they care about the job that went somewhere else. This is where certainty on the problem builds toward a 10.
Straight line step 4 — the pivot
"We don't just build your site, we manage it every month so it keeps growing instead of going stale. We charge a fraction of what a typical agency charges, and we never lock you into a contract."
Why this works No dollar figures. A number now anchors them to one tier before the audit even knows which one fits, and it's an easy detail for the brain to fixate on and later feel let down by. "Fraction of a typical agency" does the same job without writing a check we haven't diagnosed yet.
Straight line step 6 — the close
"I actually already have an email for you on file, [states the email], is that the one that reaches you best? So I can at the least send you a free audit that reviews your business's online visibility as a whole."
Why this beats "can I send you…" This isn't a request for permission, it's confirming a fact. The audit is happening either way, the only real variable is which inbox it lands in. "At the least" quietly signals more is coming, without saying the in-person ask out loud yet.

After they answer — locking it in
Still not on the clock. This is what actually finishes the call once they've responded to the email question.
If the email is correct
"Perfect. That audit will be in your inbox within twenty-four hours. Once you've had a chance to look it over, I'd love to sit down with you in person and walk through exactly what we found and how we'd fix it, that's really where this gets real. I'll follow up once it's sent to find a time that works."
If the email is wrong, or it's a front desk address
"No problem, what's the best email to reach you directly? Not the general office line, I want this landing with you, not someone else's inbox." — then go straight into the same twenty-four hour and in-person line above.
Why we always mention meeting in person The call's job isn't to close the deal, it's to earn a real conversation. Committing to an in-person walkthrough is what proves we're confident enough in what we're offering to sit across the table and back it up, not just email a pitch and hope. Reps aren't expected to close over the phone, this is the actual next step.

Why twenty-four hours, not "this week" Time is of the essence, that's the whole pitch. A specific, fast promise signals real business. A vague one sounds like every other call they've already ignored.

Analogy reference
The storefront sign is baked into the default script since it's fast to say. Swap in one of these once you're comfortable, if it fits the lead better.
Storefront sign (default)
People walk by, see you're open, but don't know what you do. That's no Google presence.
The work truck
For trades. Your website is your truck, except it's parked on every street in San Antonio at once, all day.
The phone book
For referral skeptics. Google is the phone book now, except everyone checks it constantly.
The best kept secret
Fine for a hidden gem restaurant. Not fine for a business trying to grow.
The first impression
Someone looks you up before calling. What they find decides half the outcome before you pick up.
The job that went elsewhere
The demand already exists. It's just going to whoever shows up on Google first.
Golden rules
Internalize these. They hold up on every call.
01Tell, don't ask. Every extra choice you hand the lead is control you just gave away.
02Sixty seconds, no pauses, no permission questions until the ask itself.
03Never quote an exact price on the first call. The audit decides which tier fits, not a guess.
04We build it and we manage it. Say both. One-time-build agencies are the competition, not us.
05Get a next step, not a yes. The ask is always for information, not approval.
06Leave real value even on a no. A free audit costs nothing and it's already promised.
07Follow up within 24 hours, every time. Most sales happen in the follow up.
08Know the business before you call. Google them first, walk in knowing the problem.
09Never trash the competition. Nod and pivot to what makes us different.
10Say "I'm with Boring Project," never "I run Boring Project." Only true for one person on the team.
11Human connection first, every call. Confirm the name, ask how their day's going, actually listen. Then announce the pivot yourself, don't ask if it's ok to continue.
12Confirm the email, don't ask permission to send it. A direct email beats a company inbox every time, that's who actually reads it.
13Always plant the in-person meeting. Twenty-four hours on the audit, no exceptions, time is of the essence.
Next steps after the call
The call gets the door open. These two emails are what actually keep it open until Joseph's in the room.
Reminder: this whole process exists to get Joseph, the owner and the one actually building and managing these sites, in front of the lead in person. That in-person meeting is what leads to a real close, and the close is what the 20% commission is tied to. Every step here is building toward that one meeting, not trying to replace it.

Why this matters This does two things. It's another certainty touchpoint, prompt and organized follow through is exactly what builds trust in you and the company, the second and third of the 3 Tens. And it sets a clock the lead can actually see. 24 hours is a promise, not a maybe, and a lead who's expecting something specific is still paying attention when it lands.

Talk soon, [Rep Name] Boring Project [attach the audit or paste the audit link here]
Why this matters This is the actual proof, the moment the pitch becomes something real instead of a promise. Notice the only ask here is the in-person meeting, that's on purpose. This call was never meant to close the deal on its own, it's meant to earn a seat at their table. Once Joseph is actually in the room walking through the audit and answering real questions, that's where the close happens.
One more thing, worth repeating everywhere: never mention AI, on the call or in either email. If someone asks how we build this fast, the answer is "we build faster, more efficient, and for a fraction of the cost," full stop, nothing about how. AI is a detail that scares people more than it reassures them, the client only cares about the outcome.
